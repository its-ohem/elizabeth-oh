LIFESPAN WRITING RESEARCH

Lifespan Writing Research focused on the development and evolution of writing practices across the human lifespan. This study highlights writing development in classrooms and national communities as that development interacts with age and shifting identity markers, cultural experiences, writing tools, and life events. Here, we explored how writing is not a stable universal practice, but instead takes shape (and gets re-shaped) over time, in context, and in concert with embodied experiences.

"A LIFE ON A SHELF: LESBIAN LITERACY AND LIFESPAN WRITING

“A Life on A Shelf: Lesbian Literacy and Lifespan Writing” is a project which records the experiences of Jill Lydia Ellis, a Cincinnati lesbian who wrote 25 journals from 1973-2007. With the help of three colleagues, we discovered Jill’s journals after they were donated posthumously at the Ohio Lesbian Archives. Jill’s writing is stark, personal, and arresting, and we saw a perfect opportunity for an archival exploration of her lifespan writing. Jill uses personal writing to explore her identity as a writer and a lesbian, and to work through relationships, emotions, desires, and dreams. There are stark differences in her journals over time, like changes to content, handwriting, size of text, and the level of formality. We coded various patterns that reflected the principles of lifespan writing as a field of study, particularly the exploration of self through recreational writing.

On February 28, I presented our findings at the University of Cincinnati's Graduate Student Conference: Queered Methods, Queered Stories.

This research required me to synthesize key concepts from the lifespan writing research field by analyzing how writing evolves in the self across identity, context, and lived experience. I collaborated with three other researchers in the Ohio Lesbian Archives, gaining project management experience that required the on-site digitization of materials and allocation of work. By coding Jill’s writing patterns into various themes, I was able to connect course theories about lifespan development to concrete evidence, particularly the idea that recreational writing can serve as a powerful tool for self-exploration and identity formation. Presenting our findings at the University of Cincinnati’s Graduate Student Conference further reinforced my ability to translate complex qualitative data into accessible content for a general audience.
